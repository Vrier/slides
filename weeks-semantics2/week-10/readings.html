Week 10 · Readings

Aspect & the Perfect

Reading list Figures: Klein · Dowty · Vendler

Five sources for Week 10 — a textbook chapter on tense, aspect and the aspectual classes, two classical sources (the four-way classification and the topic-time framework), and two contemporary papers (referential tense and a cross-linguistic typology of aspect). References are in APA; open-access links are given where available. Read Kearns first, then Vendler. Confirm editions and availability against the library / Blackboard list before circulating.

Reading list one textbook · two classical · two contemporary
  1. TextbookKearns, K. (2011). Tense and aspect; Aspectual classes. In Semantics (2nd ed., chaps. 8–9). Palgrave Macmillan.
    The course-text treatment — the Vendler classes, aspectual composition, and the imperfective paradox.
    Purchase
  2. ClassicalVendler, Z. (1957). Verbs and times. The Philosophical Review, 66(2), 143–160. https://doi.org/10.2307/2182371
    The four aspectual classes — states, activities, accomplishments, achievements — and the tests that distinguish them.
    Library login JSTOR
  3. ClassicalKlein, W. (1994). The expression of time: Topic time and situation time. In Time in language (chap. 6). Routledge.
    The topic-time / situation-time framework — the spine of much current work on tense and the perfect.
    Purchase
  4. ContemporaryKratzer, A. (1998). More structural analogies between pronouns and tenses. In D. Strolovitch & A. Lawson (Eds.), Proceedings of SALT 8 (pp. 92–110). CLC Publications.
    Tense as referential — the analogy with pronouns that reshaped how the field thinks about temporal reference.
    Open access Open PDF
  5. ContemporaryAltshuler, D. (2014). A typology of partitive aspectual operators. Natural Language & Linguistic Theory, 32(3), 735–775. https://doi.org/10.1007/s11049-014-9232-1
    The cross-linguistic payoff — how imperfective and progressive operators vary, and what unifies them.
    Library login DOI
