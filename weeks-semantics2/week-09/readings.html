Week 9 · Readings

Counterfactuals & Similarity

Reading list Figures: Stalnaker · Lewis · Iatridou

Five sources for Week 9 — two textbook presentations of the closest-worlds semantics, two classical sources (the selection-function and similarity-ordering accounts), and a contemporary paper on Sobel sequences and context. References are in APA; open-access links are given where available. Read a textbook treatment first, then Lewis. Confirm editions and availability against the library / Blackboard list before circulating.

Reading list two textbook · two classical · one contemporary
  1. Textbookvon Fintel, K., & Heim, I. (2011). Counterfactuals and similarity. In Intensional semantics [Lecture notes]. Massachusetts Institute of Technology.
    The closest-worlds semantics built compositionally — similarity orderings and the limit assumption.
    Open access Open PDF
  2. TextbookStarr, W. (2021). Counterfactuals. In E. N. Zalta (Ed.), The Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy (Summer 2021 ed.). Metaphysics Research Lab, Stanford University.
    The cleanest modern synthesis of the whole landscape — similarity, premise, and dynamic accounts side by side.
    Open access SEP
  3. ClassicalLewis, D. (1973). Counterfactuals (§§1.1–1.6). Blackwell.
    The similarity-ordering semantics — the system of spheres of comparative similarity around a world.
    Purchase
  4. ClassicalStalnaker, R. C. (1968). A theory of conditionals. In N. Rescher (Ed.), Studies in logical theory (pp. 98–112). Blackwell.
    The selection-function origin of the closest-world idea — one nearest world, where Lewis allows ties.
    Library login DOI
  5. Contemporaryvon Fintel, K. (2001). Counterfactuals in a dynamic context. In M. Kenstowicz (Ed.), Ken Hale: A life in language (pp. 123–152). MIT Press.
    Sobel sequences and the context-sensitivity of would — why counterfactual antecedents shift the similarity standard as discourse unfolds.
    Open access Open PDF
