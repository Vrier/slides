Week 6 · Readings

Factivity, Selection & Neg-Raising

Reading list Figures: Kiparsky² · Karttunen

Four sources for Week 6 — a textbook treatment of presupposition under embedding, two classical studies of the factive class, and a contemporary reassessment tying factivity to discourse. References are in APA; open-access links are given where available. Read von Fintel & Heim first, then the Kiparskys. Confirm editions and availability against the library / Blackboard list before circulating.

Reading list one textbook · two classical · one contemporary
  1. Textbookvon Fintel, K., & Heim, I. (2011). Presupposition projection in embedded contexts. In Intensional semantics [Lecture notes]. Massachusetts Institute of Technology.
    How a factive's presupposition behaves when embedded under attitudes and operators — the compositional baseline.
    Open access Open PDF
  2. ClassicalKiparsky, P., & Kiparsky, C. (1970). Fact. In M. Bierwisch & K. E. Heidolph (Eds.), Progress in linguistics (pp. 143–173). Mouton.
    The founding study of factive predicates — why regret presupposes its complement and believe does not.
    Purchase
  3. ClassicalKarttunen, L. (1971). Some observations on factivity. Papers in Linguistics, 4(1), 55–69. https://doi.org/10.1080/08351817109370248
    Refines the factive class — semifactives, and the contexts in which factivity weakens.
    Library login DOI
  4. ContemporaryAnand, P., & Hacquard, V. (2014). Factivity, belief and discourse. In L. Crnič & U. Sauerland (Eds.), The art and craft of semantics: A festschrift for Irene Heim (Vol. 1, pp. 69–90). MIT Working Papers in Linguistics.
    A modern reassessment — factive selection as something negotiated in discourse, not fixed in the lexicon alone.
    Open access Open PDF
