Recap & the Intensional Turn

Reading list Figures: Frege · Carnap · Montague

Three sources for Week 1 — a textbook chapter that builds the intensional framework, and two classical sources: the puzzle that motivates sense beyond reference, and the work that first treated English with an intensional logic. References are in APA; open-access links are given where available. Read von Fintel & Heim first, then Frege; Montague is hard — skim PTQ for the shape of the project. Confirm editions and availability against the library / Blackboard list before circulating.

Reading list one textbook · two classical
  1. Textbookvon Fintel, K., & Heim, I. (2011). Beginnings of intensional semantics. In Intensional semantics (chap. 1) [Lecture notes]. Massachusetts Institute of Technology.
    Extension vs. intension, possible worlds, and a compositional framework for displacement — the spine text for the term.
    Open access Open PDF
  2. ClassicalFrege, G. (1948). On sense and reference (M. Black, Trans.). The Philosophical Review, 57(3), 209–230. (Original work published 1892) https://doi.org/10.2307/2181485
    The morning / evening star — substitution failure, and why meaning must include sense, not just reference. The motivation for the whole intensional turn.
    Library login JSTOR
  3. ClassicalMontague, R. (1973). The proper treatment of quantification in ordinary English. In K. J. J. Hintikka, J. M. E. Moravcsik, & P. Suppes (Eds.), Approaches to natural language (Synthese Library, Vol. 49, pp. 221–242). Reidel. (Reprinted in P. Portner & B. H. Partee, Eds., Formal semantics: The essential readings, 2002, pp. 17–34) https://doi.org/10.1007/978-94-010-2506-5_10
    The step from an extensional to an intensional fragment — English treated with the precision of a formal language. The historical hinge; read for the shape, not every rule.
    Library login DOI
