Week 1 · Readings

What is Meaning?

Reading list Figures: Frege · Montague

Four sources for Week 1 — a textbook chapter to orient you, a classical paper that founds the field, and two contemporary surveys mapping the landscape and its history. References are in APA; open-access links are given where available. Start with the textbook chapter, then read Frege. Confirm editions and availability against the library / Blackboard list before circulating.

Reading list one textbook · one classical · two contemporary
  1. TextbookPortner, P. H. (2005). The fundamental question. In What is meaning? Fundamentals of formal semantics (chap. 1, pp. 1–30). Blackwell.
    What "mean" can mean — and why semantics settles on truth conditions.
    Purchase
  2. ClassicalFrege, G. (1948). On sense and reference (M. Black, Trans.). The Philosophical Review, 57(3), 209–230. (Original work published 1892) https://doi.org/10.2307/2181485
    The sense / reference distinction — why meaning outruns what a term picks out.
    Library login JSTOR
  3. ContemporarySpeaks, J. (2021). Theories of meaning. In E. N. Zalta (Ed.), The Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy (Spring 2021 ed.). Metaphysics Research Lab, Stanford University.
    A clean survey of the rival answers to "what is meaning?" — set the formal program in context.
    Open access SEP
  4. ContemporaryPartee, B. H. (2011). Formal semantics: Origins, issues, early impact. The Baltic International Yearbook of Cognition, Logic and Communication, 6, 1–52. https://doi.org/10.4148/biyclc.v6i0.1580
    Where the field came from: Montague, the linguistics–philosophy meeting, and why it took the formal turn.
    Open access Open PDF
