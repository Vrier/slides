I
Empirical
LENS 01 / 04 · LI7869 · Week 12

Beyond the actual world

Modal language shifts us from what is to what might, must, or is believed to be.
EmpiricalLENS 01 / 04

Modal expressions & intensionality

The expressions
Daniel is a secret agent (plain) → might be (auxiliary), Maybe (adverb), Hammurabi believes that… (attitude verb). Each shifts away from actual-world truth.
Intensionality
The top card is the Ace of Spades. Yet draw the top card ≠ draw the Ace of Spades as an instruction. Substituting co-referring terms can change meaning — the hallmark of an intensional context.
The contrast
All pedestrians obey the signal is about what happens; All pedestrians must obey the signal is about all relevant alternatives.
EmpiricalLENS 01 / 04

Flavour ambiguity & two readings

One modal, many flavours
She must be on stage: epistemic (given what we know) or deontic (given the rules). She should be home by now likewise. English leaves flavour to context.
de re vs de dicto
Clara might marry a violinist: de re — a particular violinist she might marry; de dicto — it might be that she marries some violinist or other.
Negation & modals
Priya must not share the password (¬ below: obligation not to) vs Guests cannot use the side entrance (¬ above: not permitted). Scope of negation relative to the modal varies by item.

Historical

Kripke to Kratzer

From the logic of possible worlds to a linguistic theory of context-dependent modality.
HistoricalLENS 02 / 04

Three steps to modal semantics

Lewis & Kripke
C. I. Lewis revived modal logic; Kripke (1959, 1963) gave it a possible-worlds semantics with an accessibility relation — \(\Box\) and \(\Diamond\) quantify over accessible worlds.
Hintikka
Extended worlds to attitudes: believe quantifies over doxastic alternatives, know over epistemic ones. Intensionality becomes world-quantification.
Kratzer (1981, 1991)
A unified linguistic theory: a single modal word gets its flavour from a contextual conversational background (modal base + ordering source), and its force from \(\exists\) vs \(\forall\).

Force × restriction

A modal is quantification over the worlds a conversational background makes relevant.
FormalLENS 03 / 04

Two ingredients of every modal

Force
might \(p\) is true iff \(p\) holds in some accessible world (\(\Diamond=\exists w\)); must \(p\) iff \(p\) holds in all accessible worlds (\(\Box=\forall w\)).
Restriction (flavour)
Modals quantify over the relevant worlds, set by the conversational background: epistemic (what's known), deontic (the rules), circumstantial, bouletic (desires), teleological (a goal).
Accessibility
\(\Box p\) true at \(w_0\) iff \(\forall w'[\textsc{Acc}(w_0,w') \to p(w')]\). Flavour is the choice of \(\textsc{Acc}\) — every modal needs both force and restriction.
FormalLENS 03 / 04

Worked: Clara might marry a violinist

de re — individual fixed in \(w_0\)
\exists x[\text{violinist}(x,w_0)\wedge\exists w'(\textsc{Acc}(w_0,w')\wedge\text{marry}(c,x,w'))]
de dicto — described inside the modal
\exists w'[\textsc{Acc}(w_0,w')\wedge\exists x(\text{violinist}(x,w')\wedge\text{marry}(c,x,w'))]
What differs
\text{scope of }\exists x\text{ relative to }\exists w'\text{; the world where }\textit{violinist}\text{ is evaluated}
General lesson
\textbf{de re}: x\text{ fixed in }w_0\quad\textbf{de dicto}: x\text{ varies with }w'

Typological

What modals lexicalise

English fixes force and lets context choose flavour. Some languages do exactly the reverse.
TypologicalLENS 04 / 04

The mirror image: St'át'imcets

Flavour is lexical
The epistemic clitic =k'a is only epistemic; the deontic =ka is only deontic. Flavour is fixed by the lexical item, not by context.
Force is contextual
Each item can be 'must', 'might', 'may' or 'can' depending on context — force varies, flavour does not. Exactly the reverse of English.
Why it matters
Force and restriction are separable parameters. Languages can lexicalise either one and leave the other to context — a strong argument for the Kratzerian two-part architecture.
