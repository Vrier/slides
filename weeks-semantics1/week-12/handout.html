Empirical Flavours of modality
Five flavours

epistemic (given what is known/inferred) · deontic (given rules, duties, permissions) · circumstantial (given the circumstances) · bouletic (given desires) · teleological (given a goal). English usually leaves the flavour to context.

New tool
Reading off modal flavour

Ask: is the modal interpreted given what we know, or given rules / goals / desires? Many items allow more than one flavour.

Practice · 3 tasks
1Identify the most natural flavour: Harry needs to return the book by noon. · The sunlight faded to our left, so we must be facing north. · According to the teacher, Mario cannot go on the trip.
2For Theo may be in the archive and Nadia must submit the form by noon: which flavours are available? Give a context for each.
3de re vs de dicto: paraphrase both readings of Clara might marry a violinist.
Historical From worlds to conversational backgrounds
Background

Kripke gave modal logic a possible-worlds semantics with an accessibility relation; Hintikka extended it to attitudes (believe, know). Kratzer (1981, 1991) unified the picture for natural language: one modal word gets its flavour from a contextual conversational background (modal base + ordering source) and its force from \(\exists\) vs \(\forall\).

Formal Force and restriction over worlds
Notation

\(w_0\) the actual world · \(\textsc{Acc}(w_0,w')\) "\(w'\) is accessible from \(w_0\)" · possibility \(\rightsquigarrow \exists w'\) · necessity \(\rightsquigarrow \forall w'\). might \(p\): true iff \(p\) in some belief-compatible world; must \(p\): true iff \(p\) in all.

de re / de dicto

de re fixes the individual outside the modal (in \(w_0\)); de dicto interprets the description inside it. For Clara might marry a violinist, the difference is the scope of \(\exists x\) relative to \(\exists w'\) — and the world where violinist is evaluated.

New tool
Force test

Does the expression pattern with \(\exists w\) (existential) or \(\forall w\) (universal) over worlds?

Apply it
1Classify by force: possibly, necessarily, may, can, have to, should, ought to. Flag any that are less straightforward.
2Given the de re form \(\exists x[\text{violinist}(x,w_0)\wedge\exists w'(\textsc{Acc}(w_0,w')\wedge\text{marry}(c,x,w'))]\), write the de dicto form and say what changed.
Typological Force and flavour can be lexicalised separately
Overview

In St'át'imcets (Lillooet Salish), the epistemic clitic is only epistemic and the deontic only deontic — flavour is lexically fixed — while force ('must/might/may/can') varies with context. This is the mirror image of English, where force is fixed and flavour is contextual. Force and restriction are separable.

Cross-linguistic
1Your own data:

In a language you know, pick a modal. Does it fix flavour, force, both, or neither? Give a minimal pair showing what is fixed and what varies with context.
