Week 11 · Readings

Tense and Aspect

Reading list Figures: Partee · Kratzer

Four sources for Week 11 — two textbook chapters on tense and aspect, plus a classical and a contemporary paper developing one striking idea: that tenses behave like pronouns. References are in APA; open-access links are given where available. Read the textbook chapters first, then the Partee–Kratzer pair together. Confirm editions and availability against the library / Blackboard list before circulating.

Reading list two textbook · one classical · one contemporary
  1. TextbookCoppock, E., & Champollion, L. (2024). Tense. In Invitation to formal semantics (chap. 12) [Unpublished manuscript]. Boston University & New York University.
    Tense within the formal system — temporalism vs eternalism, and what a theory of tense must deliver.
    Open access Open PDF
  2. TextbookKearns, K. (2011). Tense and aspect. In Semantics (2nd ed., chap. 9). Palgrave Macmillan.
    Reference time, the perfect, and the progressive — with Discourse Representation Theory and graded exercises.
    Purchase
  3. ClassicalPartee, B. H. (1973). Some structural analogies between tenses and pronouns in English. The Journal of Philosophy, 70(18), 601–609. https://doi.org/10.2307/2025024
    The influential claim that tenses are referential and anaphoric, just like pronouns — short and elegant.
    Library login JSTOR
  4. ContemporaryKratzer, A. (1998). More structural analogies between pronouns and tense. In D. Strolovitch & A. Lawson (Eds.), Proceedings of SALT 8 (pp. 92–110). CLC Publications.
    The modern development of Partee's idea — tenses as bound or referential variables. Read it straight after Partee.
    Open access Open PDF
