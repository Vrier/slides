I
Empirical
LENS 01 / 04 · LI7869 · Week 11

Two layers of time

Tense locates an eventuality on the timeline; aspect presents its internal temporal shape.
EmpiricalLENS 01 / 04

Distinct layers of meaning

Tense
Locates the eventuality relative to the speech time: past, present, future.
Aspect
Does not locate the eventuality; it presents it — as complete, ongoing, or already finished relative to another time.
Simple present split
With states it's present-time (She knows where we are); with non-states it's habitual (Heath bikes to work). English avoids the simple present for an ongoing eventive reading — hence the heavy use of the progressive.
EmpiricalLENS 01 / 04

The imperfective paradox

Data
Tweety was flying to London is true even if Tweety never arrived; Tweety flew to London entails arrival. Max was crossing the street when he was hit — he need not have crossed.
Why it matters
The progressive cannot mean the same as the simple clause: it presents an accomplishment without its culmination. Aspect is doing real semantic work.
Progressive & states
#Jones is owning a Jaguar is odd, but We are living in London adds a sense of temporariness over We live in London — the progressive coerces states.

Reichenbach to Klein

Three time points, tense compared to pronouns, and the rise of topic time.
HistoricalLENS 02 / 04

Building the temporal architecture

Reichenbach (1947)
The Tenses of Verbs: three points — speech \(S\), event \(E\), and reference \(R\). The perfect and the simple past differ in how \(E\), \(R\) and \(S\) line up.
Partee (1973)
Tense behaves like a pronoun: I didn't turn off the stove isn't "no past time" — it's about a salient time. Tenses and temporal adverbs are anaphoric.
Klein (1994)
Time in Language: re-centres the system on topic time \(T\) — the time the speaker is talking about. Tense relates \(T\) to \(S\); aspect relates \(E\) to \(T\).

LENS 03 / 04 · LI7869 · Week 11

S, T & E

Tense relates topic time to speech time; aspect relates event time to topic time.
FormalLENS 03 / 04

Three times, two relations

The three times
\(S\) = speech time · \(T\) = topic / reference time · \(E\) = eventuality time. Tense and aspect each fix one relation between two of them.
Tense: \(T\) vs \(S\)
past \(T < S\) · present \(T = S\) · future \(S < T\).
Topic time at work
Partee's stove: I didn't turn off the stove is evaluated at a salient past interval \(T\) — not "some time" or "ever". \(T\) is the anaphoric anchor.
FormalLENS 03 / 04

Aspect relates \(E\) to \(T\)

Simple (eventive)
E \subseteq T \quad\text{— the event falls within the topic time}
Perfect
E < T \quad\text{— the event precedes the topic time (a result holds at }T)
Progressive
T \subseteq E \quad\text{— the topic time sits inside an ongoing event}
Stative simple
T \subseteq E \ \text{or}\ E \supseteq T \quad\text{— the state holds throughout }T

Typological
LENS 04 / 04 · LI7869 · Week 11

Tenseless languages

Not every language grammaticalises tense — yet speakers still locate events in time.
TypologicalLENS 04 / 04

Aspect-prominent and tenseless systems

Tenseless languages
Mandarin has no grammatical tense; it marks aspect (perfective le, durative zhe) and leaves location-in-time to context and adverbs. The \(T\)–\(S\) relation is inferred.
Relative tense
Many languages use relative tense (anchored to a reference time, not speech time) or graded/remoteness distinctions (today's-past vs yesterday's-past) absent in English.
Moral
The \(S\), \(T\), \(E\) architecture is general; languages choose which relations to grammaticalise and which to leave to inference.
