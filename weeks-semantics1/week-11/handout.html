Empirical Tense locates; aspect presents
The split

Tense locates an eventuality relative to speech time. Aspect presents its temporal shape — complete, ongoing, already finished. The simple present is present-time with states (She knows the answer) but habitual with non-states (Heath bikes to work).

Historical Three points, then topic time
Background

Reichenbach (1947) analysed tense with speech, event, and reference points. Partee (1973) argued tense behaves like a pronoun — I didn't turn off the stove is about a salient time, not "no time ever". Klein (1994) re-centred the system on topic time \(T\): tense relates \(T\) to \(S\), aspect relates \(E\) to \(T\).

Formal The S / T / E system
Notation

\(S\) speech time · \(T\) topic/reference time · \(E\) eventuality time · \(\subseteq\) temporally includes · \(<\) temporally precedes. Tense: past \(TAspect (simple eventive): \(E\subseteq T\).

New tool
Reading off S, T, E relations

For a clause, fix the tense relation (\(T\) vs \(S\)) and the aspect relation (\(E\) vs \(T\)); the two together pin the eventuality on the timeline.

Practice · 3 tasks
1Give \(T\)–\(S\) and \(E\)–\(T\) for the simple eventive cases: Fiona rolled over · Fiona will roll over · Yesterday Tom wrote the essay.
2The perfect: Fiona has finished eating · …had finished… · …will have finished…. From the relations, what does perfect aspect contribute?
3The progressive: Tweety was flying · …is flying · …will be flying. What relation does the progressive contribute?
Typological Languages without grammatical tense
Overview

Mandarin and other tenseless languages mark aspect (e.g. perfective le) but leave the \(T\)–\(S\) relation to context and adverbs. Many languages use relative tense (anchored to a reference time) or remoteness/graded distinctions. The S/T/E architecture is general; what gets grammaticalised varies.
