I
Empirical
LENS 01 / 04 · LI7869 · Week 5

Quantified DPs aren't names

A quantified DP like every hippo doesn't denote an individual — so subject–predicate membership won't do.
EmpiricalLENS 01 / 04

The data: NPs that quantify

Data

Every / all / some / no / most hippo(s) swam.

Exactly two / fewer than five / infinitely many hippos swam.

These DPs make claims about how many — not about a particular hippo.

Why names won't do
Neil denotes an individual \(n\), and Neil swam is true iff \(n \in \sem{\text{swam}}\). But no hippo denotes no individual at all — there is nothing to drop into the set.
Inference
We want entailments — every hippo swam ⊨ every grey hippo swam — to fall out of set relations, not stipulations about special objects.

Frege to Barwise & Cooper

From quantifiers as variable-binding operators to natural-language determiners as relations between sets.
HistoricalLENS 02 / 04

Three moves to generalised quantifiers

Frege (1879)
Quantifiers as second-order operators binding variables: \(\forall x,\ \exists x\). The modern logical analysis of every and some.
Mostowski / Montague
Mid-century logic (Mostowski 1957) generalised \(\forall,\exists\) to arbitrary cardinality quantifiers; Montague (1973) put DPs and logic on one compositional footing.
Barwise & Cooper (1981)
Treat a determiner as a relation between two sets \(A\) and \(B\). This puts most, exactly two, infinitely many on a par with every — and opens the door to semantic universals.

Determiners as set relations

From \(\forall,\exists\) to set-theoretic conditions on \((A,B)\), then to generalised quantifiers as sets of sets.
FormalLENS 03 / 04

From \(\forall,\exists\) to set relations

Predicate logic
Some hippo swam: \(\exists x[\text{hippo}(x)\wedge\text{swim}(x)]\). Every hippo swam: \(\forall x[\text{hippo}(x)\to\text{swim}(x)]\).
Set equivalents
With \(A=\sem{\text{hippo}}\), \(B=\sem{\text{swim}}\):   every ⇔ \(A\subseteq B\);   some ⇔ \(A\cap B\neq\varnothing\);   no ⇔ \(A\cap B=\varnothing\).
Why this is better
most and exactly two have no first-order \(\forall/\exists\) translation, but state easily as conditions on \((A,B)\): \(|A\cap B|>|A\setminus B|\); \(|A\cap B|=2\).
FormalLENS 03 / 04

Worked: evaluate in a model

Model
A = \sem{\text{hippo}} = \{a,b,c\},\quad B = \sem{\text{swam}} = \{a,c\}
Every hippo swam
A\subseteq B?\ \ \{a,b,c\}\not\subseteq\{a,c\}\ \Rightarrow\ \textbf{false}
Some hippo swam
A\cap B=\{a,c\}\neq\varnothing\ \Rightarrow\ \textbf{true}
No hippo swam
A\cap B\neq\varnothing\ \Rightarrow\ \textbf{false}
Exactly two hippos swam
|A\cap B|=2\ \Rightarrow\ \textbf{true}
FormalLENS 03 / 04

Generalised quantifiers as sets of sets

Definition
A predicate denotes a set of individuals \(B\). A generalised quantifier denotes a set of such sets — the VP-meanings that make the DP true.
Example
Fix \(A=\sem{\text{hippo}}\).  every hippo = \(\{B : A\subseteq B\}\);  some hippo = \(\{B : A\cap B\neq\varnothing\}\).
Determiner = relation
A determiner is a condition \(\mathrm{DET}(A)(B)\) on the pair of sets. \(\sem{\text{every}}(A)(B)=[A\subseteq B]\); \(\sem{\text{no}}(A)(B)=[A\cap B=\varnothing]\).
FormalLENS 03 / 04

Conservativity & monotonicity

Conservativity
\(\mathrm{DET}(A)(B)\Leftrightarrow\mathrm{DET}(A)(A\cap B)\). To evaluate DET A are B you only look at the \(A\)s — never things outside \(A\). every: \(A\subseteq B \Leftrightarrow A\subseteq A\cap B\). ✓
Monotonicity
Upward in \(B\): \(B\subseteq C \Rightarrow [\mathrm{DET}(A)(B)\Rightarrow\mathrm{DET}(A)(C)]\). every platypus is a mammal ⊨ … an animal. no is downward; exactly two is neither.

Monotonicity does real work: downward-entailing environments are exactly where negative-polarity items (any, ever) are licensed.

Typological
LENS 04 / 04 · LI7869 · Week 5

Conservativity as a universal

Languages differ wildly in their determiner inventories — yet the determiners they lexicalise are sharply constrained.
TypologicalLENS 04 / 04

A semantic universal

The claim
Barwise & Cooper (1981): all simple natural-language determiners are conservative. No language lexicalises a determiner like only* that needs to look outside \(A\).
Why it's striking
Of the vast space of logically possible set-relations, languages draw only from the conservative ones. Variation in form; invariance in logical type.
Monotonicity & polarity
Downward-monotone determiners (no, few, fewer than three) license NPIs cross-linguistically — a second robust generalisation linking logic to grammar.
Week 5 — wrap-up

Four lenses, one picture

Empirical
Quantified DPs (every, no, most, exactly two) don't denote individuals; entailments should follow from set relations.
Historical
Frege's binding operators → Mostowski/Montague → Barwise & Cooper's determiners-as-relations.
Formal
\(\mathrm{DET}(A)(B)\) as a set condition; generalised quantifiers as sets of sets; conservativity and monotonicity.
Typological
Conservativity is a candidate universal; downward monotonicity governs NPI licensing across languages.
