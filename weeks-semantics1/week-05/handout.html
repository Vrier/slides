Empirical Why can't we treat a quantified DP as a name?
Data

Every / some / no / most / exactly two hippo(s) swam. Each says something about how many hippos swam, not about a particular hippo.

Neil swam is true iff \(n\in\sem{\text{swam}}\). But no hippo denotes no individual — there is nothing to put in the set.

Reflect
1Argue it:

Give two reasons that treating every hippo as denoting a special individual object is unattractive (think about ontology and about inference).

Historical How did determiners come to be relations between sets?
Background

Frege (1879) analysed quantifiers as operators binding variables (\(\forall,\exists\)). Mostowski (1957) generalised them to arbitrary cardinalities. Barwise & Cooper (1981) made the decisive linguistic move: a determiner denotes a relation between two sets \(A\) (the noun) and \(B\) (the predicate), so most and exactly two sit alongside every.

Formal Determiners as set-theoretic conditions on \((A,B)\)
Notation

Common nouns and VPs denote sets. \(A\) = restrictor (e.g. hippos); \(B\) = nuclear scope (e.g. swim); \(\mathrm{DET}(A)(B)\) = the truth condition of a determiner on its two set arguments.

Key entries

\(\mathbf{every}(A)(B)=[A\subseteq B]\) · \(\mathbf{some}(A)(B)=[A\cap B\neq\varnothing]\) · \(\mathbf{no}(A)(B)=[A\cap B=\varnothing]\) · \(\mathbf{most}(A)(B)=[\,|A\cap B|>|A\setminus B|\,]\).

New tool
Evaluate & derive determiner meanings

Given concrete sets you can evaluate a determiner; given an English gloss you can derive its condition using only \(\subseteq,\cap,\setminus,\varnothing,|\cdot|\).

Practice · 3 tasks
1Let \(A=\{a,b,c,d\}\), \(B=\{b,c,e\}\). Compute \(A\cap B\) and \(A\setminus B\). Is \(\mathbf{every}(A)(B)\) true? Is \(\mathbf{some}(A)(B)\) true?
2Give set-theoretic truth conditions for \(\mathbf{no}(A)(B)\), \(\mathbf{at\ least\ two}(A)(B)\), and \(\mathbf{most}(A)(B)\).
3Now derive conditions for two "fictional" determiners: \(\mathbf{exactly\ two}(A)(B)\) and \(\mathbf{between\ 3\ and\ 5}(A)(B)\).
New tool
Conservativity test

\(\mathrm{DET}\) is conservative iff \(\mathrm{DET}(A)(B)\Leftrightarrow\mathrm{DET}(A)(A\cap B)\): you only ever look at the \(A\)s. To test, intersect \(B\) with \(A\) and check the truth value is unchanged.

Apply it
1Verify that \(\mathbf{every}\) is conservative by showing \(A\subseteq B \Leftrightarrow A\subseteq A\cap B\).
2Test \(\mathbf{some}\) and \(\mathbf{no}\) for conservativity.
3Why is only (as in only hippos swam) not conservative? What does it need to look at?
Typological Conservativity and monotonicity as cross-linguistic constraints
Overview

Languages vary enormously in which determiners they lexicalise, yet all appear to obey conservativity — a candidate semantic universal. A second constraint, monotonicity, governs polarity: downward-monotone determiners (no, few) license NPIs (any, ever).

Cross-linguistic
1Monotonicity:

Define upward and downward monotonicity in \(B\). Show every is upward but not downward monotone, using mammal ⊆ animal.

2Your own data:

In a language you know, give the determiners for 'every', 'some' and 'no'. Test one for conservativity with a concrete example. Does the universal hold?
