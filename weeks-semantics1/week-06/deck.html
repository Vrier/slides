I
Empirical
LENS 01 / 04 · LI7869 · Week 6

One string, two meanings

When two operators share a clause, a single sentence can be true in two different situations.
EmpiricalLENS 01 / 04

Two quantifiers, two readings

Data
Every student read a paper.

∀ > ∃: each student read some paper or other (possibly different ones).

∃ > ∀: there is one paper that every student read.

Definition
Scope ambiguity: a single surface string maps to two distinct truth conditions, differing only in the relative order of the operators.
Note
The ambiguity is not lexical (no word is ambiguous) and not about reference — it is about configuration.
EmpiricalLENS 01 / 04

Negation and structural ambiguity

Quantifier vs negation
A student didn't pass.

∃ > ¬: there is a (specific) student who failed.

¬ > ∃: it's not the case that any student passed.

Modal vs negation
John can't leave. ¬◇ (not permitted to leave) vs ◇¬ (allowed not to leave). Which reading surfaces — and can context flip it?
Structural ambiguity
She saw the man with a telescope. The PP attaches either to the VP (instrument) or to the NP (modifier) — different trees, different meanings.

Historical
LENS 02 / 04 · LI7869 · Week 6

From quantifying-in to LF

How semanticists gave scope a home — a covert syntactic level where operator order is fixed.
HistoricalLENS 02 / 04

Two ways to give scope a level

Montague (1973)
Quantifying-in: a derivational rule inserts a quantified DP at the top of the clause, leaving a bound variable below. Scope = order of insertion.
May (1977, 1985)
Quantifier Raising at Logical Form (LF): a covert movement adjoins each quantifier above the clause. The two attachment heights give the two readings directly.
Reinhart
Later work (Reinhart 1997 and others) constrains QR — not every scope is available, and processing/economy plays a role. Scope is grammatically regulated, not free.

Formal
LENS 03 / 04 · LI7869 · Week 6

Relations & operator order

Transitives denote relations; quantifiers target argument positions; scope is the order in which operators apply.
FormalLENS 03 / 04

Transitives are relations

Definition
A transitive verb denotes a set of ordered pairs: \(\sem{\text{love}}=\{\langle x,y\rangle : x\text{ loves }y\}\), type \(\langle e,\langle e,t\rangle\rangle\). Either argument can host a quantifier.
Examples
Every dog is happy: \(\forall x[\text{dog}(x)\to\text{happy}(x)]\).
John loves every dog: \(\forall x[\text{dog}(x)\to\text{love}(j,x)]\).
Some dog chased some cat: \(\exists x[\text{dog}(x)\wedge\exists y[\text{cat}(y)\wedge\text{chase}(x,y)]]\).
FormalLENS 03 / 04

Worked: Every student read a paper

Scope-takers
\text{every student}\ (\forall),\qquad \text{a paper}\ (\exists)
Surface reading — ∀ > ∃
\forall x[\text{stu}(x)\to\exists y(\text{paper}(y)\wedge\text{read}(x,y))]
Inverse reading — ∃ > ∀
\exists y[\text{paper}(y)\wedge\forall x(\text{stu}(x)\to\text{read}(x,y))]
How they differ
\exists{>}\forall \models \forall{>}\exists,\ \text{but not conversely}

The inverse reading is logically stronger: one shared paper guarantees each-read-some, but not vice versa.

FormalLENS 03 / 04

Negation as a scope-taker

Sentence
\text{A student didn't pass.}
∃ > ¬ (specific)
\exists x[\text{stu}(x)\wedge\neg\,\text{pass}(x)]
¬ > ∃ (none)
\neg\,\exists x[\text{stu}(x)\wedge\text{pass}(x)]
Modal parallel
\neg\Diamond\,\text{leave}\quad\text{vs}\quad\Diamond\,\neg\,\text{leave}

Typological
LENS 04 / 04 · LI7869 · Week 6

Rigid vs flexible scope

Languages differ in how freely operators may be reordered — evidence that scope tracks structure.
TypologicalLENS 04 / 04

Structure constrains scope

Mandarin — měi…dōu
měi-yī-běn shū yǒu yī-ge rén dōu mǎi-le · 'every-book, one person dōu bought' → the existential takes wide scope: there is a person who bought every book.
Scope rigidity
Mandarin is largely scope-rigid: surface order fixes scope, where English allows inverse scope. Word order and markers like dōu do the work covert QR does in English.
Moral
Cross-linguistic patterns show scope is sensitive to structural configuration and grammatical cues — not free interpretation. Neg-raising and modals give further contrasts.
