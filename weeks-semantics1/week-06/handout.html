Historical How did scope get a syntactic home?
Background

Montague (1973) handled scope with quantifying-in: a rule that introduces a quantified DP high in the derivation, binding a lower variable. May (1977, 1985) recast this as Quantifier Raising at a covert level, Logical Form: the two attachment heights yield the two readings. Later work (Reinhart) constrains which scopes are actually available.

Formal Relations, logical forms, and operator order
Definition

A transitive verb denotes a relation: \(\sem{\text{love}}=\{\langle x,y\rangle : x\text{ loves }y\}\). Scope is the relative order of operators (\(\forall,\exists,\neg,\Diamond\)) in the logical form; reordering them gives the readings.

Key idea

\(\forall x[\text{stu}(x)\to\exists y(\text{paper}(y)\wedge\text{read}(x,y))]\) (∀>∃) is entailed by \(\exists y[\text{paper}(y)\wedge\forall x(\text{stu}(x)\to\text{read}(x,y))]\) (∃>∀), but not conversely. The inverse reading is logically stronger.

Progression · from prose to logical form
Translate into predicate logic, using dog(·) cat(·) swim(·) chase(·,·) etc. Where a sentence is scopally ambiguous, give both logical forms.
(a)

Every dog is happy.

(b)

John loves every dog.

(c)

Some dog chased some cat.

(d)

Every student read a paper. — both readings.

Typological How freely can languages reorder operators?
Overview

English allows inverse scope fairly freely; Mandarin is largely scope-rigid — surface order fixes scope, and markers like dōu regulate quantifier interaction. Cross-linguistic patterns show scope tracks structural configuration, not free interpretation.
