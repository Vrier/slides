EmpiricalLENS 01 / 04

Alternations and the psych puzzle

Dative
gave a bone to the puppy / gave the puppy a bone, but *donated the shelter a bone. Verbs in a class share alternations; near-synonyms may not.
Spray–load & causative
sprayed paint on the wall / sprayed the wall with paint (not *poured the wall); John broke the vase / the vase broke.
Psych verbs
Midge loves ice cream (experiencer subject) vs The article enraged Thomas (experiencer object). Same two participants, opposite syntax.

HistoricalLENS 02 / 04

A lineage of thematic theory

Fillmore (1968)
The Case for Case: every clause has an inventory of deep "cases" (Agentive, Instrumental, Dative…) mapped to surface form.
Gruber & Jackendoff
Localist roles: theme, goal, source and path generalise spatial motion to change and possession, encoded in Lexical Conceptual Structure.
Baker (1988)
UTAH: identical thematic relations are mapped to identical syntactic positions — a bridge between meaning and structure.
Dowty (1991)
Proto-roles: not discrete labels but cluster concepts — Proto-Agent and Proto-Patient entailments — with argument selection by entailment count.

FormalLENS 03 / 04

Three principles of realisation

Theta Criterion
Each argument bears exactly one theta-role, and each theta-role is assigned to exactly one argument. A one-to-one match between roles and positions.
UTAH
A given theta-role is uniformly associated with the same kind of syntactic position — so systematic thematic structure is mirrored systematically in syntax.
Burzio's Generalisation
A verb assigning no external argument assigns no accusative case: John broke the vase / the vase broke / *broke the vase. Role structure constrains case.
FormalLENS 03 / 04

Roles as positions in structure

Agent
x\ \text{in}\ \textsc{do}(x,[\dots])
Causer
x\ \text{in}\ x\ \textsc{cause}\ [\dots]
Theme (change of state)
y\ \text{in}\ \textsc{become}[\,P(y)\,]
Goal
z\ \text{in}\ \textsc{become}[\textsc{be-at}(y,z)]
Object-experiencer
x\ \textsc{cause}\ [\textsc{become}[\textsc{feel}(y,\text{state})]]

In neo-Davidsonian terms each label is a role predicate: The storm broke the window = \(\exists e[\textsc{break}(e)\wedge\textsc{causer}(e,s)\wedge\textsc{theme}(e,w)]\).

FormalLENS 03 / 04

Reinhart's two features

Role[c] cause[m] mental
Agent++
Causer / Instrument+−
Experiencer−+
Theme / Patient−−

Decompose each role into [±c] (causes change) and [±m] (sentient). When two positions are open, the higher role takes the structurally higher one — predicting subject- vs object-experiencer patterns.

Mapping roles to syntax

If UTAH is right, the role-to-position mapping should recur across languages — modulo their case systems.
TypologicalLENS 04 / 04

One hierarchy, many case systems

Thematic hierarchy
Agent > recipient/benefactive > theme > location/source/goal — a recurring ranking that predicts which role becomes subject and which object.
Case strategies
Nominative–accusative vs ergative–absolutive systems realise the same roles differently. Dative subjects (Icelandic, Hindi) show experiencers needn't be nominative.
Psych verbs cross-linguistically
Subject- vs object-experiencer splits recur widely; Reinhart's [±c]/[±m] aims to predict the variation from a single feature system.
