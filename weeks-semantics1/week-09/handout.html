Empirical What does a verb's participant structure look like?
Role labels

Agent volitional controller · Causer brings about change, non-volitional · Theme moves/changes/is located · Patient affected · Goal/Source/Path endpoint/start/route · Recipient/Benefactive possessor reached / intended · Experiencer feels or perceives · Stimulus is perceived or provokes.

New tool
Reading off thematic roles

For each DP, ask: does it act, undergo change, mark an endpoint, or feel something? Then label it. Severed into neo-Davidsonian form, Sarah threw the branch at the window = \(\exists e[\textsc{throw}(e)\wedge\textsc{agent}(e,s)\wedge\textsc{theme}(e,b)\wedge\textsc{goal}(e,w)]\).

Practice · 3 tasks
1Label the roles, then give neo-Davidsonian forms: The vase broke. · The faucet dripped water onto the floor.
2Selena sent Luke a postcard. · Maya baked Leo a cake. — identify recipient vs benefactive.
3All of the hikers trudged along the ridge to the lodge from their camp. — label every PP (path, goal, source).
Historical How did role theory get from labels to entailments?
Background

Fillmore (1968) proposed deep "cases"; Gruber & Jackendoff developed localist roles (theme, goal, source, path) in Lexical Conceptual Structure; Baker (1988) formulated UTAH; Dowty (1991) replaced discrete labels with Proto-Agent and Proto-Patient entailment clusters, deriving argument selection by counting entailments.

Formal LCS decomposition and role predicates
LCS schemas

agent = \(x\) in \(\textsc{do}(x,[\dots])\) · causer = \(x\) in \(x\ \textsc{cause}\ [\dots]\) · theme = \(y\) in \(\textsc{become}[P(y)]\) · goal = \(z\) in \(\textsc{become}[\textsc{be-at}(y,z)]\) · object-experiencer = \(x\ \textsc{cause}\ [\textsc{become}[\textsc{feel}(y,\text{state})]]\).

Key idea

The Theta Criterion (one role per argument, one argument per role) and UTAH (same role → same position) link decomposition to syntax. Burzio's Generalisation ties accusative case to having an external argument.

New tool
LCS ↔ neo-Davidsonian

Read a role off a structural position in the LCS, then realise it as a role predicate of the event.

Apply it
1For each LCS, give (i) an English verb, (ii) the main roles, (iii) a neo-Davidsonian form: \(x\ \textsc{cause}[\textsc{become}[\text{broken}(y)]]\); \(\textsc{do}(x,[\text{walk}(x)])\); \(x\ \textsc{cause}[\textsc{become}[\textsc{be-at}(y,z)]]\).
2Causative/inchoative: give forms for John broke the vase and The vase broke, and state the entailment.
Typological Reinhart's features and the psych-verb puzzle
Overview

Reinhart decomposes roles into [±c] (causes change) and [±m] (mental): Agent [+c,+m], Causer [+c,−m], Experiencer [−c,+m], Theme [−c,−m]. When two positions are open, the higher role takes the higher position — predicting subject- vs object-experiencer realisation across languages.

Cross-linguistic
1Classify:

For The article enraged Thomas, Midge loves ice cream, and The poltergeist frightened John: is the non-experiencer more a cause or a target? Do realisations track Reinhart's features?

2Your own data:

In a language you know, give a subject-experiencer and an object-experiencer psych verb. How is each participant case-marked?
