I
Empirical
LENS 01 / 04 · LI7869 · Week 8

The adverb problem

Adverbs and adjunct PPs drop freely and entail the unmodified sentence — a fact a good semantics should make unsurprising.
EmpiricalLENS 01 / 04

Adverbs drop, entailments follow

Data

Jones buttered the toast slowly ⊨ Jones buttered the toast.

… slowly, in the kitchen, with a knife ⊨ Jones buttered the toast.

Any subset of adjuncts may be dropped; the inference is automatic.

The bad fix
Adding an argument place per adverb — \(\textsc{butter}(x_1,\dots,x_n)\) for speed, time, place, instrument, beneficiary… — gives an unbounded, implausible lexical entry.
Argument vs adjunct
Arguments are selected, often obligatory, not freely repeatable. Adjuncts are optional and freely stackable. The semantics should respect this contrast.
EmpiricalLENS 01 / 04

Language treats events like things

Pronouns
John buttered the toast. Mary did it too. The pronoun it refers back to an event.
Quantification
Everything John did was rude. We quantify over things John did — plausibly events.
Perception
John saw Mary buttering the toast. What John saw is an event — the buttering itself.

If events are referred to, quantified over, and perceived, they earn a place in our ontology.

II
Historical
LENS 02 / 04 · LI7869 · Week 8

Davidson & the neo-Davidsonians

One short 1967 paper put events into logical form; a later generation pulled the arguments out of the verb.
HistoricalLENS 02 / 04

Two generations of event semantics

Davidson (1967)
The Logical Form of Action Sentences: action verbs carry a hidden event argument \(e\). Adverbs are predicates of \(e\); the entailments fall out as conjunct-dropping.
Castañeda / Parsons
The neo-Davidsonian turn (Castañeda 1967; Parsons 1990): even the subject and object leave the verb, re-entering as thematic-role predicates of the event.
Why it stuck
Events gave compositional, modular meanings for adjuncts and a uniform treatment of participants — now the standard ontology for verbal semantics.
III
Formal
LENS 03 / 04 · LI7869 · Week 8

An event variable

Add \(\exists e\) to the verb; let adjuncts be conjoined predicates of \(e\); optionally sever the arguments.
FormalLENS 03 / 04

Worked: adjuncts as conjuncts

Sentence
\text{Jones buttered the toast in the kitchen with a knife.}
Davidsonian LF
\exists e[\textsc{butter}(e,j,t)\wedge \textsc{in}(e,k)\wedge \textsc{with}(e,n)]
Drop a conjunct
{}\models\ \exists e[\textsc{butter}(e,j,t)\wedge \textsc{in}(e,k)]
Drop all adjuncts
{}\models\ \exists e[\textsc{butter}(e,j,t)]

Each adjunct is one conjunct; conjunction elimination delivers every entailment for free.

FormalLENS 03 / 04

Severing the arguments

Davidsonian
\(\exists e[\textsc{butter}(e,j,t)\wedge \textsc{slow}(e)]\) — subject and object stay inside the verb.
Neo-Davidsonian
\(\exists e[\textsc{butter}(e)\wedge \textsc{agent}(e,j)\wedge \textsc{theme}(e,t)\wedge \textsc{slow}(e)]\) — every participant is a thematic-role predicate of \(e\).
Pay-off
Arguments and adjuncts become parallel. Causative/inchoative pairs entail directly: \(\dots\wedge\textsc{agent}(e,m)\wedge\textsc{theme}(e,d)\models\dots\wedge\textsc{theme}(e,d)\).
FormalLENS 03 / 04

What the two analyses predict

Spray–load
load the truck with hay / load the hay onto the truck: neo-Davidsonian gives one core — \(\textsc{agent}(e,m)\wedge\textsc{theme}(e,h)\wedge\textsc{goal}(e,tr)\) — capturing the shared meaning.
Passives
The door was opened deliberately still implies an agent: \(\exists x\,\exists e[\textsc{open}(e)\wedge\textsc{agent}(e,x)\wedge\textsc{theme}(e,d)]\). The agent is existentially bound, not deleted.
Limits
Agent-oriented (deliberately) and speaker-oriented (unfortunately) adverbs resist the simplest "predicate-of-event" treatment — events are necessary, not sufficient.
IV
Typological
LENS 04 / 04 · LI7869 · Week 8

Overt event structure

Many languages spell out, in their morphology, exactly the thematic-role predicates the neo-Davidsonian posits.
TypologicalLENS 04 / 04

Morphology spells out roles

Case marking
Ergative, accusative, dative, instrumental cases overtly tag a participant's relation to the event — a morphological \(\textsc{agent}(e,x)\), \(\textsc{instrument}(e,y)\).
Applicatives
Bantu and many other languages add a verbal affix that introduces an extra participant (benefactive, locative) — adding a thematic-role conjunct in the morphology.
Serial verbs
Serial-verb languages describe one complex event with several verbs sharing arguments — direct support for a single event variable underlying the clause.
Week 8 — wrap-up

Four lenses, one picture

Empirical
Droppable adjuncts and their entailments; events are referred to, quantified over and perceived.
Historical
Davidson's 1967 event argument; the neo-Davidsonian severing of arguments (Parsons).
Formal
\(\exists e[\dots]\) with adjuncts as conjuncts; thematic roles; alternations, causatives and passives.
Typological
Case, applicatives and serial verbs make event participants and thematic relations overt.
