Historical How did part–whole logic come to replace flat sets for nouns?
Background

Mereology — the logic of parts and wholes (Leśniewski 1916; Leonard & Goodman, Calculus of Individuals, 1940) — offered an alternative primitive to set membership: part-of.

Quine (1960) identified cumulative reference as the hallmark of mass terms: any sum of water is water. Link (1983) then built a single lattice-structured domain — atomic for count, atomless for mass — that is still the standard model.

Reflect

2Why it mattered:

Link's move was to give individuals internal structure. What can a lattice domain express that a set of unstructured points cannot? Answer in two or three sentences.

Formal Orders, sums, atoms, and cumulativity
Definition

A partial order \(\le\) is reflexive, antisymmetric and transitive. A join-semilattice adds a sum \(+\) that is associative, commutative and idempotent, inducing the order \(x \le y \Leftrightarrow x+y=y\). Read \(\le\) as part-of.

Key idea

Cumulativity: \(P(x)\wedge P(y)\Rightarrow P(x{+}y)\). Mass and plural predicates are cumulative; singular count is not. Atoms: count nouns bottom out in bounded atoms; mass nouns are downward-closed and atomless.

Typological Where, across languages, does unithood get realised?
Overview

Numerals always require units. English supplies them via count/mass lexicalisation, plural morphology and measure phrases (three glasses of water). Classifier languages (Mandarin sān zhī gǒu) and numeral-plus-singular languages (Turkish üç kitap) place the unit elsewhere. The semantics — atoms, sums, units — is constant.
