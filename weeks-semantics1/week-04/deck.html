Empirical
LENS 01 / 04 · LI7869 · Week 4

Three ways of individuating

Count, mass and plural nouns differ systematically in how they combine with numerals, quantity words, and agreement.
EmpiricalLENS 01 / 04

Count vs mass: the diagnostics

Count nouns

Combine with numerals — three rings.

Take a/an — a ring.

Pluralise — ring / rings.

Mass nouns

Take much / little — much water, a little wine.

Resist bare numerals — *three water (need a measure).

Resist count plural — water / *waters, unless shifted.

Key idea
The contrast is grammatical, not about the world: furniture is mass, chairs is count, though both describe discrete objects.
EmpiricalLENS 01 / 04

Plurals look two ways at once

Built from atoms
horses is built from the count atom horse — it inherits bounded units.
…yet pattern with mass
a lot of horses / a lot of water · more horses / more water · enough horses / enough water. The same quantity words span plural and mass.
Agreement
Collective nouns split dialects: American English prefers the team has won; many Irish/British/Canadian speakers also allow the team have won.
The target
We want one semantics that captures both the atomhood of plurals and their mass-like cumulativity.
EmpiricalLENS 01 / 04

Why predicates-as-sets isn't enough

What we had
A flat membership claim: \(a \in \sem{\text{dog}}\). Individuals are unanalysed points with no internal parts.
What mass & plurality need

Parts and sums — how entities combine and divide.

Bounded atoms vs no privileged atoms.

Structure-sensitive operations: combine (+) and split.

→ The domain of individuals needs internal part–whole structure. That is the formal job of this week.

II
Historical
LENS 02 / 04 · LI7869 · Week 4

Link and the mereological turn

How part–whole logic, dormant since Leśniewski and Quine, became the standard semantics of plurals and mass terms.
HistoricalLENS 02 / 04

A part–whole tradition

Leśniewski & Goodman
Mereology — the formal logic of parts and wholes (Leśniewski, 1916; Leonard & Goodman's Calculus of Individuals, 1940) — gave a rigorous alternative to set membership.
Quine (1960)
Cumulative reference: any sum of water is water. Quine isolated the property that distinguishes mass terms — and that sets, on their own, cannot express.
Link (1983)
Godehard Link unified count plurals and mass nouns in a single lattice-structured domain: atoms for count, atomless join-semilattices for mass. The framework we use today.

Formal
LENS 03 / 04 · LI7869 · Week 4

Lattices, sums & atoms

Partial orders give us part-of; join gives us sums; cumulativity and atoms separate mass from count.
FormalLENS 03 / 04

From an order to a lattice

Partial order
A relation \(\le\) that is reflexive (\(x \le x\)), antisymmetric (\(x\le y \wedge y\le x \Rightarrow x=y\)), and transitive (\(x\le y \wedge y\le z \Rightarrow x\le z\)).
Lattice
A poset in which every pair has a join \(x \sqcup y\) (least upper bound) and a meet \(x \sqcap y\) (greatest lower bound). Intuition: \(\sqcup\) is like union, \(\sqcap\) like intersection.
For nouns
Read \(\le\) as part-of and write the join as a sum \(+\). Noun denotations become regions of a part-of lattice.
FormalLENS 03 / 04

Sum is a join-semilattice

Associative
\((x + y) + z = x + (y + z)\)
Commutative
\(x + y = y + x\)
Idempotent
\(x + x = x\)
Induces the part-of order
\(x \le y \;\Leftrightarrow\; x + y = y\)

These three laws are exactly what we need: order and sum are two views of one structure.

FormalLENS 03 / 04

A part-of lattice: three horses

World \(w_3\) with exactly three horses \(A,B,C\):

\(\sem{\text{horse}}_{w_3} = \{A,\,B,\,C\}\)  — the atoms.

\(\sem{\text{horses}}_{w_3} = \{A{+}B,\ A{+}C,\ B{+}C,\ A{+}B{+}C\}\)  — the proper sums.

the horses picks the maximal sum \(A{+}B{+}C\).

FormalLENS 03 / 04

Cumulativity and bounded atoms

Cumulative
Mass and plural predicates are closed under sum: \(\text{water}(x) \wedge \text{water}(y) \Rightarrow \text{water}(x{+}y)\); likewise horses. Singular count is not: \(\text{horse}(x)\wedge\text{horse}(y) \not\Rightarrow \text{horse}(x{+}y)\).
Splitting
Split a plural and you eventually hit an atom — a bounded "one horse". Split mass (water) and every part is still water: no privileged atom.
The split
Count nouns come with bounded atoms; mass nouns are atomless (downward-closed). One lattice domain, two regimes.
FormalLENS 03 / 04

Two productive shifts

Universal Grinder
Count → mass. Put any object through the grinder: There was dog all over the road. The atoms are erased; the predicate becomes downward-closed.
Universal Packager
Mass → count. Impose units: two waters, three coffees = two/three contextually standard portions. Atoms are supplied by the context.
Material vs artefact
A sum-preserving map \(\mathit{mat}\) sends a ring to its gold, a statue to its clay: \(\mathit{mat}(x{+}y)=\mathit{mat}(x)+\mathit{mat}(y)\). Same matter, different counting.

Typological
LENS 04 / 04 · LI7869 · Week 4

Where unithood is realised

Numerals always need units — but languages supply them in strikingly different places.
TypologicalLENS 04 / 04

Three places to put the unit

English
Unithood in the lexicon (count vs mass), in plural morphology, and in measure phrases — three glasses of water.
Mandarin — classifier
sān zhī gǒu · 'three cl dog' = 'three dogs'. The noun is number-neutral; the classifier supplies the counting unit.
Turkish — numeral + singular
üç kitap · 'three book' = 'three books'. The unit is present, just not spelled out as plural morphology.
TypologicalLENS 04 / 04

One semantics, many morphologies

Classifier vs measure
A classifier counts discrete units already individuated by the noun; a measure word (cup, kilo, litre) counts amounts. The split tracks count vs mass.
Beyond speech
The mass/count contrast is not tied to the speech modality: sign languages encode analogous unithood and individuation distinctions with classifier-like resources.
Moral
The semantic architecture — atoms, sums, parts, units — stays stable; only where and how it is realised varies.
