Stuff and Things

Reading list Figures: Link · Chierchia

Three sources for Week 4 — a textbook chapter on referring expressions, a classical source for the lattice-theoretic account of plurals and mass terms, and a contemporary paper on how the mass / count divide varies across languages. References are in APA; open-access links are given where available. Link is demanding — read the textbook chapter first, then take Link slowly. Confirm editions and availability against the library / Blackboard list before circulating.

Reading list one textbook · one classical · one contemporary
  1. TextbookPortner, P. H. (2005). Complexities of referring expressions. In What is meaning? Fundamentals of formal semantics (chap. 5). Blackwell.
    Definites, indefinites, and especially mass and bare-plural nouns — the data this week explains.
    Purchase
  2. ClassicalLink, G. (1983). The logical analysis of plurals and mass terms: A lattice-theoretical approach. In R. Bäuerle, C. Schwarze, & A. von Stechow (Eds.), Meaning, use, and interpretation of language (pp. 302–323). de Gruyter.
    The founding mereological account — sums, parts, and the lattice structure behind plural and mass reference.
    Purchase
  3. ContemporaryChierchia, G. (1998). Reference to kinds across languages. Natural Language Semantics, 6(4), 339–405. https://doi.org/10.1023/A:1008324218506
    The typological payoff — why classifier languages like Chinese treat all nouns as mass-like (the Nominal Mapping Parameter).
