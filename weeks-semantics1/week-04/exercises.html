Conventions: \(\le\) is an order, usually read as part-of; \(\sqcap\) = meet (greatest lower bound), \(\sqcup\) = join (least upper bound); \(+\) is sum of individuals (the join under part-of). Show your reasoning — a correct value with no justification earns half marks. Draw diagrams where asked.

1 Count, mass, plural diagnosticsEmpirical 5 marks

For each noun, mark whether it accepts each frame (✓/✗) and give its class.

(a)marble — in the frames three ___, much ___, ___s. Note: marble has two readings. Identify both.
(b)furniture, rice, committee — class each, and say which is grammatically mass despite naming discrete objects.
(c)Give two quantifiers that combine with both plural count and mass nouns, and one that combines with only one class.
2 Subsets ordered by \(\subseteq\)Formal 5 marks

Let \(S\) be the non-empty subsets of \(\{1,2,3\}\), ordered by \(\subseteq\).

(a)List all members of \(S\) and draw the Hasse diagram.
(b)Compute the joins \(\{1\}\sqcup\{2\}\), \(\{1\}\sqcup\{1,2\}\), \(\{2,3\}\sqcup\{3\}\) (if they exist in \(S\)).
(c)Give a pair whose meet fails to exist in \(S\), and explain why. What single element could you add so every pair has a meet? Is the result a lattice?
3 Divisibility latticeFormal 4 marks

Let \(S=\{1,2,3,4,6,12\}\) ordered by divisibility (\(x\le y\) iff \(x\mid y\)).

(a)Draw the Hasse diagram.
(b)Compute \(4\sqcap 6\), \(4\sqcup 6\), \(3\sqcap 4\), \(3\sqcup 4\), \(2\sqcap 12\), \(2\sqcup 12\) within \(S\).
(c)Which elements are minimal? Which is maximal? In one sentence, what do meet and join correspond to here?

4 A plural latticeFormal 6 marks

Fix a world \(w\) with exactly four atomic hippos: \(\sem{\text{hippo}}_w=\{A,B,C,D\}\). Allow all sums; read \(x\le y\) as x is part of y.

(a)Draw the part-of structure: four atoms plus all non-atomic sums. List the elements that cover \(A\) (its immediate supersums).
(b)Give denotations for \(\sem{\text{hippos}}_w\), \(\sem{\text{exactly two hippos}}_w\), \(\sem{\text{less than three hippos}}_w\), and \(\sem{\text{the hippos}}_w\) (definite = maximal sum).
(c)Compute \((A{+}B)\sqcap(B{+}C)\), \((A{+}B)\sqcup(B{+}C)\), and \((A{+}C)\sqcap(C{+}D)\). Is each result an atom, a sum, or neither?
5 Cumulativity & downward closureEmpirical 5 marks

Let \(G\) be a portion of mud with proper parts \(G_1,G_2\) and \(G=G_1+G_2\). Assume downward closure: \(\text{mud}(x)\wedge y\le x \Rightarrow \text{mud}(y)\).

(a)If \(\text{mud}(G)\), what follows about \(\text{mud}(G_1)\), \(\text{mud}(G_2)\)? If \(G_1=H_1+H_2\), what about \(H_1,H_2\)?
(b)Explain why downward closure conflicts with there being a linguistically privileged "one mud" atom.
(c)Compare more gravel and more marbles: what does each normally compare (volume/weight vs cardinality)? Give a context where more marbles is judged by weight.
6 Material, atoms & unithood across languagesTypological 5 marks

Let statue be count and clay mass, with a sum-preserving material map \(\mathit{mat}(x{+}y)=\mathit{mat}(x)+\mathit{mat}(y)\). Define \(\text{Atom}_P(x)\) as: \(P(x)\) and \(x\) has no proper \(P\)-parts.

(a)Describe a scenario where two distinct statues \(S_1\ne S_2\) have the same material \(\mathit{mat}(S_1)=\mathit{mat}(S_2)\). Does sum-preservation rule it out? Propose one extra constraint on \(\mathit{mat}\) that would.
(b)Write a definition of \(\text{Atom}_P(x)\) using \(\le\) and \(P\). Which of marble, furniture, rice, committee, sand plausibly have semantic atoms?
(c)Say 'three dogs' and 'three (glasses of) water' in a language you know; gloss both. Where is the counting unit realised — noun, classifier, plural morphology, or measure word?
