Four classes, six tests

Verbs differ in the inner temporal shape of the situations they describe — and the difference is grammatically real.
EmpiricalLENS 01 / 04

The four aspectual classes

ClassstaticdurativetelicExamples
State++−know French, love Fiona, be tall
Activity−+−run, swim, push a cart
Accomplishment−++write an essay, build a house
Achievement−−+notice the mistake, reach the summit

Three binary features — static, durative, telic — cut the space into four.

EmpiricalLENS 01 / 04

Diagnostics — and a court case

for / in adverbials
ran for an hour (atelic) vs wrote the essay in an hour (telic). finish and agentive/speed adverbs further separate the classes.
Habitual present
Arnold runs (habitual) vs Arnold is afraid of spiders (stative, non-habitual). The simple present sorts statives from non-statives.
Nielsen v. Preap (2019)
A statute applies "when the alien is released". Is is released an achievement (a moment) or a state (a span)? The whole case turns on this aspectual classification.

Aristotle to Vendler

The telic/atelic distinction is ancient; the modern four-way classification is sixty years old.
HistoricalLENS 02 / 04

From Aristotle to incremental themes

Aristotle
kinēsis (a movement with an end — telic) vs energeia (an activity complete at every moment — atelic). The first statement of the telic/atelic split.
Vendler (1957)
Verbs and Times: the four classes — states, activities, accomplishments, achievements — defined by temporal behaviour with the progressive and time adverbials.
Dowty & Krifka
Dowty (1979) decomposed the classes with do, become, cause; Krifka (1989, 1998) tied telicity to incremental themes via mereology.

Features & measuring-out

The subinterval property distinguishes homogeneous from culminating eventualities; incremental themes measure out the event.
FormalLENS 03 / 04

Homogeneity & the subinterval property

Definition
\(P\) has the subinterval property iff whenever \(P\) holds of an interval \(I\), it holds of every subinterval of \(I\). States and activities have it; accomplishments and achievements do not.
Why it matters
If you ran for an hour, then you ran in any sub-stretch (homogeneous, atelic). If you wrote an essay in an hour, no proper sub-stretch is a writing-the-essay (culminating, telic).
Structure of telics
Accomplishments: preparation → culmination → result. Achievements: (near-instantaneous) culmination → result. The endpoint is part of the meaning.
FormalLENS 03 / 04

Objects measure out events

The contrast
drink wine / eat apples (atelic) vs drink a glass of wine / eat the apple (telic). A bounded object bounds the event.
Incremental theme
With an incremental theme, progress through the object tracks progress through the event — a structure-preserving map (homomorphism) from object parts to event parts (Krifka).
Compositional aspect
Aktionsart is not just lexical: the object's boundedness (recall Week 4's atoms and sums) helps determine the telicity of the whole VP.

Typological
LENS 04 / 04 · LI7869 · Week 10

Marking telicity

English leaves telicity largely to the object; other languages mark it on the verb or with case.
TypologicalLENS 04 / 04

Three ways to grammaticalise aspect

Slavic perfectivity
Russian, Polish and others mark perfective vs imperfective on the verb, often by prefix — grammaticalising boundedness/completion directly in the verbal morphology.
Finnish object case
Accusative object → telic (the whole thing); partitive object → atelic/ongoing. Case on the object signals event boundedness, an overt incremental-theme effect.
Moral
The same aspectual categories recur, but languages locate the marking differently — on the verb, on object case, or (as in English) in the object's boundedness.
