New tool
The diagnostic battery

Six tests separate the classes: for/in adverbials (atelic/telic), the habitual present, the finish test, agentive adverbs (carefully/deliberately), speed adverbs (quickly/slowly), and when-clauses.

Practice · 3 tasks
1Judge and explain: Carla ran for / in fifteen minutes; Carla arrived for / in fifteen minutes; Carla did her homework for / in fifteen minutes. State the for/in generalisation.
2Which of Arnold recognises the car / runs / fears spiders get a habitual reading in the simple present? Form a generalisation.
3With which classes can finish combine? Test finished recognising the car / crossing the street / writing the essay / being afraid.
Historical Where the classes come from
Background

Aristotle distinguished kinēsis (telic movements) from energeia (atelic activities complete at every moment). Vendler (1957) gave the modern four-way classification; Dowty (1979) decomposed it with do, become, cause; Krifka linked telicity to incremental themes.

Formal The subinterval property and measuring-out
Definition

\(P\) has the subinterval property iff: if \(P\) holds of interval \(I\), then \(P\) holds of every subinterval of \(I\). States and activities have it (homogeneous, atelic); accomplishments and achievements do not (they culminate).

Key idea

An incremental theme measures out the event: progress through the apple tracks progress through eat the apple. A bounded object yields a telic VP — object boundedness (Week 4) feeds event telicity.

Apply it
1Show the effect:

Explain why eat apples is atelic but eat the apple is telic, using the subinterval property. Give the for/in judgement that confirms each.

Typological Where telicity is marked
Overview

English leaves telicity largely to object boundedness. Slavic languages mark perfective vs imperfective on the verb (often by prefix); Finnish uses object case (accusative = telic, partitive = atelic). Same categories, different locus of marking.
