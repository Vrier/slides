Implicature

Reading list Figures: Grice

Three sources for Week 3 — a textbook chapter on Gricean implicature, the classical paper that founded the theory, and a contemporary paper bringing it into formal focus. References are in APA; open-access links are given where available. Read the textbook chapter first, then Grice. Confirm editions and availability against the library / Blackboard list before circulating.

Reading list one textbook · one classical · one contemporary
  1. TextbookGrundy, P. (2008). Implicit meaning: Grice's theory of conversational implicature. In Doing pragmatics (3rd ed.). Hodder Education.
    The Cooperative Principle and the maxims, worked through everyday examples and exercises.
    Purchase
  2. ClassicalGrice, H. P. (1975). Logic and conversation. In P. Cole & J. L. Morgan (Eds.), Syntax and semantics 3: Speech acts (pp. 41–58). Academic Press.
    The founding statement — the Cooperative Principle, the maxims, and how implicatures are calculated.
    Purchase
  3. ContemporarySauerland, U. (2004). Scalar implicatures in complex sentences. Linguistics and Philosophy, 27(3), 367–391. https://doi.org/10.1023/B:LING.0000023378.71748.db
    The modern formal turn — how quantity implicatures behave when sentences get complex.
    Open access Open PDF
