Week 8 · Readings

Politeness

Reading list Figures: Brown · Levinson

Five sources for Week 8 — two textbook treatments of politeness, two classical sources (the face-based model and its rule-based predecessor), and a contemporary critique of the universalist programme. References are in APA; open-access links are given where available. Read Grundy first, then Brown & Levinson. Confirm editions and availability against the library / Blackboard list before circulating.

Reading list two textbook · two classical · one contemporary
  1. TextbookGrundy, P. (2008). Politeness phenomena. In Doing pragmatics (3rd ed.). Hodder Education.
    Face, face-threatening acts, and the positive / negative politeness strategies — with data and exercises.
    Purchase
  2. TextbookCulpeper, J., & Haugh, M. (2014). Im/politeness. In Pragmatics and the English language. Palgrave Macmillan.
    A current, data-rich treatment that also brings impoliteness into view.
    Purchase
  3. ClassicalBrown, P., & Levinson, S. C. (1987). Politeness: Some universals in language usage (§§1–3). Cambridge University Press. (Original work published 1978)
    Face, FTAs, and the politeness super-strategies — the model the whole field still argues with.
    Purchase
  4. ClassicalLakoff, R. (1973). The logic of politeness; or, minding your p's and q's. In Papers from the Ninth Regional Meeting of the Chicago Linguistic Society (pp. 292–305). Chicago Linguistic Society.
    The first rules-of-politeness proposal — politeness as a Gricean-style system of conversational rules.
    Purchase
  5. ContemporaryHaugh, M. (2007). The discursive challenge to politeness research: An interactional alternative. Journal of Politeness Research, 3(2), 295–317. https://doi.org/10.1515/PR.2007.013
    The case against universal politeness — politeness as something negotiated in interaction, not predicted by strategy.
    Library login DOI
