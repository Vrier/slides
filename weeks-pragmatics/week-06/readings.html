Reading list Figures: Shannon · Zipf

Four sources for Week 6 — a textbook chapter on language models, two classical sources (the founding theory of information, and the empirical law behind word frequency), and a contemporary review tying them to our Irish case study. References are in APA; open-access links are given where available. Read Jurafsky & Martin first. Confirm editions and availability against the library / Blackboard list before circulating.

Reading list one textbook · two classical · one contemporary
  1. TextbookJurafsky, D., & Martin, J. H. (2024). N-gram language models. In Speech and language processing (3rd ed. draft).
    Probability, entropy, and perplexity for language — the toolkit for the Irish word-frequency case study.
    Open access Open online
  2. ClassicalShannon, C. E. (1948). A mathematical theory of communication. The Bell System Technical Journal, 27(3), 379–423. https://doi.org/10.1002/j.1538-7305.1948.tb01338.x
    The founding paper — entropy, redundancy, and the channel. §§1 and the discussion of English suffice.
    Open access Open PDF
  3. ClassicalZipf, G. K. (1949). Human behavior and the principle of least effort (chap. on word frequency). Addison-Wesley.
    The empirical law behind the case study — a few words do most of the work, in every language.
    Purchase
  4. ContemporaryPiantadosi, S. T. (2014). Zipf's word frequency law in natural language: A critical review and future directions. Psychonomic Bulletin & Review, 21(5), 1112–1130. https://doi.org/10.3758/s13423-014-0585-6
    What Zipf's law does and doesn't explain — the modern reassessment, directly relevant to the Irish data.
    Open access Open PDF
