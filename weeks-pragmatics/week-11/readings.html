Week 11 · Readings

Rational Speech Acts

Reading list Figures: Lewis · Frank · Goodman

Five sources for Week 11 — two textbook surveys of the Rational Speech Act framework, a classical source for its signalling-game roots, and two contemporary papers (the founding model, and the conceptual case for a Bayesian pragmatics). References are in APA; open-access links are given where available. Read a survey first, then Frank & Goodman. Confirm editions and availability against the library / Blackboard list before circulating.

Reading list two textbook · one classical · two contemporary
  1. TextbookGoodman, N. D., & Frank, M. C. (2016). Pragmatic language interpretation as probabilistic inference. Trends in Cognitive Sciences, 20(11), 818–829. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.tics.2016.08.005
    The compact review that doubles as the teaching text — listener, speaker, and the Bayesian recursion between them.
    Library login DOI
  2. TextbookDegen, J. (2023). The Rational Speech Act framework. Annual Review of Linguistics, 9, 519–540. https://doi.org/10.1146/annurev-linguistics-031220-010811
    The most current survey — what RSA has explained, and the open problems it still faces.
    Library login DOI
  3. ClassicalLewis, D. (1969). Signalling. In Convention: A philosophical study (chap. 4). Harvard University Press.
    The signalling game RSA formalizes — how meaning can arise from coordinated, self-interested choice.
    Purchase
  4. ContemporaryFrank, M. C., & Goodman, N. D. (2012). Predicting pragmatic reasoning in language games. Science, 336(6084), 998. https://doi.org/10.1126/science.1218633
    The founding RSA paper — a one-parameter model that predicts speaker and listener behaviour in reference games.
    Open access Open PDF
  5. ContemporaryFranke, M., & Jäger, G. (2016). Probabilistic pragmatics, or why Bayes' rule is probably important for pragmatics. Zeitschrift für Sprachwissenschaft, 35(1), 3–44. https://doi.org/10.1515/zfs-2016-0002
    The fullest conceptual case for the approach — why Bayesian inference is the right shape for pragmatic reasoning.
    Library login DOI
