Week 12 · Readings

Grammaticalism

Reading list Figures: Chierchia · Fox

Four sources for Week 12 — two textbook treatments (the canonical statement of the grammatical view, and a chapter placing it against its rivals), the classical paper that launched the programme, and a contemporary survey of alternative-based meaning. References are in APA; open-access links are given where available. Read Chierchia, Fox & Spector first. Confirm editions and availability against the library / Blackboard list before circulating.

Reading list two textbook · one classical · one contemporary
  1. TextbookChierchia, G., Fox, D., & Spector, B. (2012). Scalar implicature as a grammatical phenomenon. In C. Maienborn, K. von Heusinger, & P. Portner (Eds.), Semantics: An international handbook of natural language meaning (Vol. 3, pp. 2297–2331). de Gruyter Mouton.
    The canonical case for computing scalar implicature in the grammar — the exhaustivity operator and its alternatives.
    Purchase
  2. TextbookHuang, Y. (2007). The semantics–pragmatics interface. In Pragmatics. Oxford University Press.
    Grammaticalism set against the neo-Gricean and relevance-theoretic alternatives — what's at stake in the debate.
    Purchase
  3. ClassicalChierchia, G. (2004). Scalar implicatures, polarity phenomena, and the syntax/pragmatics interface. In A. Belletti (Ed.), Structures and beyond: The cartography of syntactic structures (Vol. 3, pp. 39–103). Oxford University Press.
    The paper that launched the programme — implicatures interacting with polarity in ways a purely pragmatic theory can't predict.
    Purchase
  4. ContemporaryGotzner, N., & Romoli, J. (2022). Meaning and alternatives. Annual Review of Linguistics, 8, 213–234. https://doi.org/10.1146/annurev-linguistics-031120-122328
    A capstone survey — how alternatives unify implicature, presupposition, and focus across the field.
