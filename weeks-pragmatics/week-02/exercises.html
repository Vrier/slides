Six problems. Speech-act analysis is about what an utterance does, not whether it is true — so judge utterances as felicitous / infelicitous, not true/false, and always state which felicity condition is at stake. Use \(F(p)\) for the force–content split (illocutionary force \(F\) applied to propositional content \(p\)).

Empirical Historical Formal Applied
1 Performative or constative?Empirical 5 marks

For each utterance, decide whether it is a performative (the saying is the doing) or a constative (a report that can be true or false). Apply the hereby-test and note the result.

(a)I promise to email you the notes.
(b)The notes are on Blackboard.
(c)I name this ship the Beagle.
(d)I warn you that the step is loose.
2 Misfires & felicity conditionsEmpirical 5 marks

Each scenario describes an utterance that misfires. Identify the felicity condition that fails (e.g. authority, sincerity, uptake, appropriate circumstances) and name Austin's category (misinvocation / misexecution / abuse).

(a)A passer-by with no legal standing announces to a couple, "I now pronounce you married."
(b)Someone says "I promise to repay you" while fully intending never to.
(c)A referee says "Play on" but no players hear it over the crowd.
3 The force–content split \(F(p)\)Formal 6 marks

Decompose each utterance into its illocutionary force \(F\) and its propositional content \(p\). Where two utterances share \(p\) but differ in \(F\), say so.

(a)You will close the door.  ·  Close the door!  ·  Will you close the door?
(b)Write the three above in \(F(p)\) notation, sharing one \(p\).
(c)Why does the force–content split explain that these three "say the same thing" yet do different things? One or two sentences.

4 Searle's five illocutionary typesFormal 5 marks

Classify each utterance as Assertive · Directive · Commissive · Expressive · Declaration, and give the direction of fit (words-to-world, world-to-words, both, or none).

(a)I'll have the essay in by Monday.
(b)Congratulations on the result!
(c)The meeting is adjourned. (said by the chair)
(d)Hand in your phones.
5 Austin & Searle at OxfordHistorical 5 marks

Short written answers — two to four sentences each.

(a)Austin began with a performative/constative distinction but ended up abandoning it. What did he replace it with, and why was the original distinction unstable?
(b)What did Searle systematise that Austin left as observation? Name one advantage and one cost of the more formal taxonomy.
6 Speech acts in the wildApplied 4 marks

Declarations depend on institutional authority; dogwhistles exploit the gap between literal content and uptake to stay deniable.

(a)Give one real institutional declaration (legal, academic, religious, or sporting) and state exactly which authority and circumstances make it felicitous.
(b)Explain how a dogwhistle lets a speaker perform one act for an in-group while denying it to others. Which felicity dimension is being manipulated?
Optional · Challenge
★ Indirect speech acts bonus

"Can you pass the salt?" is literally a question about ability but is used as a request. Sketch how the literal force and the intended force relate, and why the hearer recovers the request rather than answering "yes".
