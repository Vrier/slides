The question behind this week

How do we do things with words?

Some utterances do not describe the world at all. They change it.
The received picture
An utterance reports a state of affairs, and we judge it true or false. Week 1 already strained this picture: much of what we say is not description.
This week's claim
Saying can be doing. Some utterances are actions, and the right question is not whether they are true but whether they succeed.

STRAND 01 / 04 · LI7862 · Week 2

When is saying also doing?

Performatives and felicity: utterances we judge successful or failed acts in context, not true or false descriptions.
What is this utterance doing?
I now pronounce you husband and wife.an officiant, at a wedding
Empirical

Saying it makes it so

I now pronounce you husband and wife.an officiant, at a wedding
Not a report
You cannot reply That's false. Nothing is being described: before the words there is no marriage, and after them there is one.
Performative
Austin's name for utterances that perform the very act they name. In saying I pronounce, the officiant pronounces.
More performatives · first person, present tense
(1)
I apologize.
(2)
I promise to pay you tomorrow.
(3)
I hereby declare the meeting open.the hereby test: a performative still sounds natural with hereby inserted
What is this utterance doing?
I bet you sixpence it will rain tomorrow.J. L. Austin's own example
Empirical

A bet needs taking

I bet you sixpence it will rain tomorrow.
Uptake
The bet exists only once you answer You're on. Some acts need the hearer's uptake to come off at all.
Evaluation
If it rains I win, and if not you do. But the bet itself was never true or false; it was on, or it was off.
In effect, or not in effect. Never true, never false.
What is this utterance doing?
I hereby declare you married.someone at a house party, glass raised
Empirical

Nothing happened

I hereby declare you married.someone at a house party, glass raised
What went wrong
Not falsity. There is no recognised procedure for house-party weddings, the speaker has no standing, and everyone treats it as a joke: the act simply fails to come off.
The diagnosis
The utterance is infelicitous. To say exactly when performatives fail, Austin wrote out conditions of felicity.
Empirical

Austin's felicity conditions

Six conditions, lettered A, B and Γ. Break one, and the act misfires or is abused.
A · The procedure
A.1
There must exist an accepted conventional procedure with a conventional effect, including the uttering of certain words by certain persons in certain circumstances.
A.2
The particular persons and circumstances must be appropriate for the procedure invoked.
B · The execution
B.1
The procedure must be executed correctly…
B.2
…and completely.
Γ · The spirit
Γ.1
Participants must have the thoughts, feelings and intentions the procedure requires…
Γ.2
…and must subsequently conduct themselves accordingly.
Empirical

Misfires and abuses

Which condition breaks determines how the act fails.
A or B broken
Misfire
The act is null and void: it does not come off at all.
I hereby declare you married.the house-party wedding: no procedure, no authority. No marriage exists.
Γ broken
Abuse
The act comes off, but hollow: performed without the required spirit.
I promise to pay you tomorrow.said with no intention of paying. Insincere, but the promise was made, and you may hold me to it.
The lesson
Performatives have their own dimension of assessment: not truth, but felicity. That dimension has structure.

Historical
STRAND 02 / 04 · LI7862 · Week 2

Austin & Searle

Post-war Oxford ordinary-language philosophy, and the move from Austin's disruptive observations to Searle's systematic theory.
Historical

Ordinary-language Oxford

After the war, Oxford philosophers treated everyday usage as serious evidence about meaning.
The milieu
Austin, Grice and Strawson, with Searle arriving as a Rhodes Scholar: careful attention to what we actually say, when we would say it, and what saying it does.
Against the positivists
Where logical positivism dismissed unverifiable sentences as meaningless (week 1), the ordinary-language school asked what those sentences were for.
Saturday mornings
Austin ran weekly term-time discussions where philosophers worked through usage together: collective, meticulous, and suspicious of grand theory.
Historical

Austin: saying as doing

JLA
J. L. Austin
1911 – 1960 · Oxford
John Langshaw Austin was an Oxford ordinary-language philosopher, trained as a classicist at Balliol. During the war he led the intelligence section preparing for the D-Day landings, with a meticulousness colleagues considered decisive. He published almost nothing in his lifetime; his lectures were reconstructed and published after his early death.
How to Do Things with Words
The 1955 William James Lectures at Harvard, published posthumously in 1962. They disrupt the received picture: many utterances are actions performed in speaking, not descriptions to be judged true or false.
The collapse
Austin begins by separating performatives from truth-evaluable constatives, then shows the border will not hold: stating is itself an act, with its own ways of failing. Every utterance does something.
Historical

Searle: the systematiser

John Searle
1932 – 2025 · Berkeley
John Searle came to Oxford from the United States as a Rhodes Scholar and studied at Christ Church while Austin was lecturing. He spent his career at Berkeley, where his later work ranged over intentionality, consciousness (the Chinese Room), and institutional facts. Where Austin observed, Searle built theory.
Speech Acts (1969)
Turned Austin's observations into a theory: every illocution analysed as a force applied to a content, governed by statable conditions.
The taxonomy (1976)
Classified every possible illocutionary act into five types, using three diagnostic dimensions. The formal strand works through both.

STRAND 03 / 04 · LI7862 · Week 2

Force, content & fit

Separating what an utterance is about from what it does: three acts, the force/content split, and the five illocutionary types.
Formal

One utterance, three acts

Austin splits any utterance into three simultaneous acts.
There's a bull in that field.
Locutionthe act of saying
Producing the words, with their sense and reference.
She uttered an English sentence about a bull and a field.
“She said that there is a bull in that field.”
Illocutionthe act in saying
What the utterance counts as: its force.
Speech-act theory lives here. This is the act we classify.
“She warned me not to cross.”
Perlocutionthe act by saying
The effects the utterance has on the hearer.
Not conventional: the same warning may reassure or terrify.
“She talked me into taking the long way round.”
Formal

Force over content

Every illocution splits into what it is about and what it does. Searle writes F(p): a force applied to a content.
one content p
Sam smokes
habitually
⟶
⊢(p)
Assertion
Sam smokes habitually.
?(p)
Question
Does Sam smoke habitually?
!(p)
Order
Sam, smoke habitually!
W(p)
Wish
Would that Sam smoked habitually.
Why it matters
Same content, four different acts. Force is not part of p; it is what the speaker does with p. Searle's example, from Speech Acts.
Formal

Direction of fit

When words and world disagree, which one is at fault?
↓
Word to world
The words must match the world. If they do not, the words are wrong: retract the claim.
assertions · predictions
↑
World to word
The world must be made to match the words. If it is not, the world is at fault: the order was disobeyed, the promise broken.
requests · promises
0
No fit
The fit is presupposed. The point is to express an attitude toward a content taken for granted.
thanks · apologies
Second dimension
Alongside the illocutionary point and the sincerity condition (the state expressed: belief, want, intention), direction of fit is one of Searle's three diagnostics for the taxonomy.

Formal

Indirect speech acts

Can you pass me the salt?
Two acts at once
Literally a question about ability (the secondary act); in use, a polite request (the primary act). The hearer computes the request from the question, plus context.
Spot the primary act
(1)
It's cold in here.→ a request: close the window
(2)
Do you know what time it is?→ a request: tell me the time
(3)
I wouldn't do that if I were you.→ a warning
Why indirect?
Indirectness leaves the hearer an out: they can respond to the literal act. This is where speech acts meet politeness, in week 8.

STRAND 04 / 04 · LI7862 · Week 2

Speech acts in the wild

From institutions that run on declarations to dogwhistles engineered for deniability: speech-act theory as a tool for analysing real communication.
Applied

Institutions run on declarations

Whole institutions exist because the right person can say the right words in the right setting.
Declarations at work
(1)
I resign.
(2)
You're out.an umpire says it, and the batter is out
(3)
The meeting is adjourned.
(4)
I sentence you to five years.only a judge, only in court, only after a verdict
The infrastructure
Each works only inside a procedure with recognised authority: Austin's A-conditions, hardened into standing institutions.
Institutional facts
Searle's later point: money, marriage, property and games exist partly because we declare them, and keep treating them as declared.
Applied

Dogwhistles as communicative acts

An utterance engineered to perform different acts for different audiences.
Overt
The target meaning is accessible to all hearers, but its significance goes unrecognised by outsiders.
Covert
The target meaning itself is concealed from at least part of the audience.
The common core
Audience segmentation plus deniability: both kinds rely on concealment from some hearers, and the speaker can always retreat to the innocent reading.
Read as speech acts
One locution, two intended illocutions: an innocuous act for the broad audience, a signal of allegiance for the target audience.
Applied

Defusing, misfire, backfire

Publicly naming a dogwhistle can defuse it, but only under conditions: felicity conditions, again.
Norm acceptance
Defusing works only where the audience shares a norm against explicit prejudice, so that exposure carries a cost.
Target match
…and only where the audience agrees on who the target is. Protean dogwhistles shift targets, so both conditions wobble and defusing fails.
Misfire
The target audience misses the signal: nothing beyond the innocent reading is communicated. The covert act does not come off.
Backfire
The broad audience recognises the signal: deniability collapses, and the speaker owns the act in public.
