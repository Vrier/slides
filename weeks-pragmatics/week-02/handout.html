Empirical Performatives, felicity, and misfires
Data

Some utterances do not describe the world — in saying them you perform an act: I apologize · I promise to pay you tomorrow · I hereby declare the meeting open.

Such a performative can misfire without being false. We evaluate it for felicity, not truth: is there an appropriate procedure, do participants have the right authority, is it executed correctly and completely, with the right intention and follow-through?

New tool
Felicity conditions

A performative succeeds only if its felicity conditions hold. Five common failure points: procedure (is there a recognised practice?), authority (right roles/standing?), sincerity (right intention?), uptake (do others take it up as that act?), and follow-through (is it completed?).

Felicity debugging · for each case, name what fails & propose a minimal fix
1 A student says: I hereby award you a First.
2 A stranger says I forgive you — to someone they have never met.
3 Someone says I promise to help you tomorrow while making clear they have no intention to help.
4 A spectator says You're out! in a sport they are not officiating.
Historical Two figures of ordinary-language philosophy
Background

J. L. Austin (1911–1960) — Oxford philosopher, wartime intelligence; How to Do Things with Words (1962, posthumous). Disrupts the assumption that utterances primarily describe and are true/false: many are actions performed in speaking.

John Searle (1932–2024) — Oxford then Berkeley; gave a systematic taxonomy of illocutionary acts (1976), plus work on intentionality (the Chinese Room) and institutional facts. Both belong to a milieu — with Grice and Strawson — that treats everyday usage as serious evidence.

Reflect
1In your own words:

Why was Austin's claim — that many utterances are actions, not descriptions — a disruption to the picture of language as primarily true-or-false description?

2Critical engagement:

Searle wants classification that is stable across surface forms and languages. Give one reason a theory built from a list of performative verbs (apologise, promise, …) is inadequate by comparison.

Formal Force over content, and three diagnostic dimensions
Definition

Separate propositional content from illocutionary force: every illocution is F(p). Classify the force on three dimensions — point (what it is for), direction of fit (↓ word-to-world, ↑ world-to-word, 0 none), and sincerity (B belief, W want, I intention).

New tool
Locution / illocution / perlocution

Austin's three layers: the locutionary act (an utterance with a form and content), the illocutionary act (what is performed in saying it — asserting, requesting, warning), and the perlocutionary effects (what is brought about — persuading, embarrassing). One locution, one illocution, many possible perlocutions.

Separate the layers · locution · illocution · two plausible perlocutionary effects
1 A: Are you free to talk?  B: I'm walking into a meeting.
2 That was a brave thing to say.
3 (in court) I object.

Applied Institutions, authority, and dogwhistles
Overview

Institutions run on speech acts whose force depends on authority and procedure (declarations, verdicts, pronouncements). Dogwhistles (Saul) exploit the gap between audiences: a target audience takes up a message while a broader public is managed. Overt vs covert turns on whether the target meaning is accessible; protean dogwhistles let the target shift across hearers (e.g. immigration talk in the Brexit campaign).

Apply it
1Speech acts in institutions:

Choose one setting (court, hospital, university, workplace). Identify three speech acts central to how it functions; for each, state what authority/role is required, what counts as uptake, and what makes the act challengeable (procedurally or evidentially).

2Analyse a political case:

Take one public utterance about immigration, borders, or "ordinary people". Decide whether it best fits overt, covert, or protean dogwhistling and justify. Then say what its Norm Acceptance and Target Match conditions are, and describe a plausible misfire or backfire.
