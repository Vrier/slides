Week 5 · Readings

Common Ground & Projection

Reading list Figures: Stalnaker · Heim

Four sources for Week 5 — a textbook chapter on presupposition and implicature in the common ground, a classical statement of common ground, and two contemporary accounts of projection. References are in APA; open-access links are given where available. Read Potts first, then Stalnaker. Confirm editions and availability against the library / Blackboard list before circulating.

Reading list one textbook · one classical · two contemporary
  1. TextbookPotts, C. (2015). Presupposition and implicature. In S. Lappin & C. Fox (Eds.), The handbook of contemporary semantic theory (2nd ed., pp. 168–202). Wiley-Blackwell.
    Common ground, projection, and the at-issue / not-at-issue split — the cleanest map of the territory.
    Purchase
  2. ClassicalStalnaker, R. (2002). Common ground. Linguistics and Philosophy, 25(5–6), 701–721. https://doi.org/10.1023/A:1020867916902
    The mature statement — common ground as iterated mutual acceptance, and the context set it defines.
    Library login DOI
  3. ContemporarySchlenker, P. (2009). Local contexts. Semantics and Pragmatics, 2(3), 1–78. https://doi.org/10.3765/sp.2.3
    The influential dynamic account of how presuppositions project, derived rather than stipulated.
    Open access Open PDF
  4. ContemporaryTonhauser, J., Beaver, D., Roberts, C., & Simons, M. (2013). Toward a taxonomy of projective content. Language, 89(1), 66–109. https://doi.org/10.1353/lan.2013.0001
    The empirical typology — not all projective content behaves alike, shown with cross-linguistic data.
    Library login DOI
