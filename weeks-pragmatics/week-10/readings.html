Week 10 · Readings

Relevance Theory

Reading list Figures: Sperber · Wilson

Six sources for Week 10 — two textbook introductions, two classical sources founding Relevance Theory, and two contemporary extensions into the cognitive science of communication. References are in APA; open-access links are given where available. Read a textbook chapter first, then Sperber & Wilson. Confirm editions and availability against the library / Blackboard list before circulating.

Reading list two textbook · two classical · two contemporary
  1. TextbookWilson, D., & Sperber, D. (2004). Relevance theory. In L. R. Horn & G. Ward (Eds.), The handbook of pragmatics (pp. 607–632). Blackwell.
    The authors' own canonical statement — ostensive-inferential communication and the comprehension procedure.
    Purchase
  2. TextbookGrundy, P. (2008). Implicit meaning: Sperber and Wilson's Relevance Theory. In Doing pragmatics (3rd ed.). Hodder Education.
    A gentler route in, with worked examples and exercises — pairs with the Grice chapter from Week 3.
    Purchase
  3. ClassicalSperber, D., & Wilson, D. (1995). Relevance: Communication and cognition (2nd ed., chaps. 1 & 3). Blackwell. (Original work published 1986)
    The founding work — relevance as the principle that drives inference, and how comprehension follows it.
    Purchase
  4. ClassicalCarston, R. (2002). Thoughts and utterances: The pragmatics of explicit communication (chap. 1). Blackwell.
    The explicature — what RT adds to Grice's said / implicated divide; the pragmatics of explicit content.
    Purchase
  5. ContemporarySperber, D., Clément, F., Heintz, C., Mascaro, O., Mercier, H., Origgi, G., & Wilson, D. (2010). Epistemic vigilance. Mind & Language, 25(4), 359–393. https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1468-0017.2010.01394.x
    Communication requires trust — how hearers stay vigilant against misinformation while still cooperating.
    Library login DOI
  6. ContemporaryMercier, H., & Sperber, D. (2011). Why do humans reason? Arguments for an argumentative theory. Behavioral and Brain Sciences, 34(2), 57–74. https://doi.org/10.1017/S0140525X10000968
    The provocative extension — reasoning evolved for persuasion, not solitary truth-seeking.
    Library login DOI
